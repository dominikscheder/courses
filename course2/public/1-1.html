1.1 Definitions

Definition 1.1.1 (Tseitin formulas). Let $G = (V,E)$ be an undirected graph and let $s : V \rightarrow \{0,1\}$, called the parity requirements. The Tseitin system for $G$ and $s$ is a system of linear equations over $GF(2)$ with one variable $x_e$ for each $e \in E$ and a constraint $c_u$ for each $u \in V$:

$$ \begin{align*} \sum_{e: u \in e} x_e \equiv s(u) \mod 2 \tag{$c_u$}\ . \end{align*} $$

Example 1.1.2 Let us consider the following system of four equations on five variables defined by the following graph and parity requirement:

Vertices without a 1 next to them are considered to have a parity requirement of $0$. Let's write the system of equations:

$$ \begin{align*} h + i&= 1 \tag{$c_t$}\\ e + f&= 1 \tag{$c_u$} \\ f + g + i&= 0 \tag{$c_v$}\\ e + g + h&= 0 \tag{$c_w$} \end{align*} $$

We write $e + f$ instead of $x_e + x_f$ to increase readability. This system of equations has a solution, for example

$$ \begin{align*} (e,f,g,h,i) = (1,0,1,0,1) \ . \end{align*} $$

Let's look at the same graph but with different parity requirements:

The system of equations is as the above, just the right-hand side of $c'_u$ is now $0$:

$$ \begin{align*} h + i&= 1 \tag{$c'_t$}\\ e + f&= 0 \tag{$c'_u$} \\ f + g + i&= 0 \tag{$c'_v$}\\ e + g + h&= 0 \tag{$c'_w$} \end{align*} $$

This system of equations is unsatisfiable. Adding up all constraints gives us

$$ \begin{align} h + i + e + f + f + g + i + e + g + h = 1 \label{eqn-sum-all-constraints} \end{align} $$

Every variable occurs twice on the left-hand side; this is clear since for each edge $e = \{u,v\}$, the variable $x_e$ appears in $c_u$ and $c_v$ and nowhere else. Modulo 2 the above (\ref{eqn-sum-all-constraints}) reduces to

$$ \begin{align*} 0 = 1 \ , \end{align*} $$

which is clearly unsatisfiable.

A vector $\x \in \{0,1\}^E$ can be interpreted as a spanning subgraph $G_x = (V,E_x)$ of $G$ with $E_x := \{e \in E \ | \ x_e = 1\}$. This $\x$ is a solution to the Tseitin system if and only if the degree of each $v \in V$ in $G_x$ is odd if $s(v)=1$ and even if $s(v)=0$.

$$ \begin{align*} x_{\{a,b\}} + x_{\{a,d\}}&= 1 \tag{$c_a$} \\ x_{\{a,b\}} + x_{\{b,c\}} + x_{\{b,d\}}&= 0 \tag{$c_b$} \\ x_{\{b,c\}} + x_{\{c,d\}}&= 0 \tag{$c_c$} \\ x_{\{a,d\}} + x_{\{b,d\}} + x_{\{c,d\}} + x_f&= 0 \tag{$c_d$} \end{align*} $$

Adding them up (and reducing modulo 2) gives us

$$ \begin{align*} x_f = 1 \end{align*} $$

because every edge inside $S = \{a,b,c,d\}$ appears twice and gets canceled. Edges like $\{u,v\}$ outside $S$ don't appear at all; edges between $S$ and $V \setminus S$ appear exactly once. In this example, this is just $f$.

Definition 1.1.4 (Boundary). Let $G = (V,E)$ be an undirected graph and $S \subseteq V$ be a set of vertices. The edge boundary of $S$ is

$$ \begin{align*} \delta(S) := \{e \in E \ | \ |e \cap S| = 1 \} \ . \end{align*} $$

Note that $\delta(S) = \emptyset$ if and only if $S$ is the union of connected components (possible the empty union of zero connected components).

Observation 1.1.5 Let $S \subseteq V$. When we add up the constraints $c_u$ for all $u \in S$, i.e., when building $\sum_{u \in S} c_u$, we obtain the equation

$$ \begin{align*} \sum_{e \in \delta(S)} x_e \equiv \sum_{u \in S} s(u) \mod 2 \end{align*} $$

Proof As argued in the above example: when an edge $f = \{u,v\}$ is completely inside $S$, i.e., when $u, v \in S$, then $x_f$ appears twice in the sum over the constraints; if $f$ lies completely outside $S$, i.e., $u, v \not \in S$, then $x_f$ doesn't appear at all; if $f \in \delta(S)$ then $x_f$ appears once.A\(\square\)

From linear algebra we know (or should know) that a system of equations $A \x = \s$ is unsolvable if and only if there exists a row vector $\r^T$ with $\r^T \cdot A = \mathbf{0}$ and $\r^T \cdot \s = 1$. In our case, where everything is modulo 2, the product $\r \cdot A$ just means selecting a subset $S$ of constraints and adding them up, by setting $S := \{v \in S \ | \ r_v = 1\}$. By Observation 1.1.5 this means that

$$ \begin{align*} \mathbf{0} = \r^T \cdot A&= \sum_{e \in \delta(S)} x_e \\ 1 = \r^T \cdot \s&= \sum_{u \in S} s(u) \end{align*} $$

The first equation implies that $S$ is a union of connected components; the second one says that the number of vertices in $S$ that have parity requirement 1 is odd. From this it follows that there is at least one connected component $S'$ with an odd number of parity-requirement-1 vertices:

Lemma 1.1.6 The Tseitin system is satisfiable if and only if every connected component has an even number of vertices with parity requirement $1$.

Proof The only if direction is clear: if a connected component $S$ has an odd number of parity-1-vertices, then adding up the constraints $c_u$ for all $u \in S$ gives us $0=1$, so the system is clearly unsatisfiable.

For the if part we can use the linear algebra reasoning from above; or we can prove it directly: First, if $G$ has multiple connected components, we can construct a solution for each component separately. So let's assume that $G$ is connected. Let us look at the vertices of parity requirement 1 and call them

$$ \begin{align*} v_1, v_2, \dots, v_l \ . \end{align*} $$

By assumption, $l$ is even. We put them in pairs:

$$ \begin{align*} (v_1, v_2), (v_3, v_4), \dots, (v_{l-1}, v_l) \ . \end{align*} $$

For each such pair $(v_{2i-1}, v_{2i})$ we find a path $p$ between them; this exists because $G$ is connected by assumption. We consider $p$ as a subgraph of $G$. We take $l/2$ such paths, connecting each pair. Then we "add up the paths" as described above and obtain a subgraph that satisfies all constraints.A\(\square\)
