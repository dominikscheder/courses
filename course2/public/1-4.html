1.4 Playing the Atserias-Dalmau game on an expander

Atserias-Dalmau games offer an alternative route to proving that Tseitin formulas $\tseitin(G,s)$ on an expander graph $G$ have large resolution width.

Theorem 1.4.1 Let $G=(V,E)$ be a connected $(t,h)$-expander and let $s: V \rightarrow \{0,1\}$ be a parity requirement function with $\sum_{v \in V} s(v) \equiv 1 \mod 2$. Then $F := \tseitin(G,s)$ is unsatisfiable and Prover can win the game $\adg(F,w)$ for $w := ht/2-1$

Together with Theorem 1.3.2 from the previous chapter, this implies that any resolution refutation of $F$ will need width at least $ht/2$. This constitutes another, alternative proof of Theorem 1.2.5.

Proof Delayer maintains at all times a total assignment $\alpha$ with the following invariants:

  1. $\alpha$ is a superassignment of $\rho$. That is, $\rho(x_e) \in \{\alpha(x_e), \texttt{undefined}\}$ for all edges $e \in E$.

  2. $\alpha$ satisfies all constraints $c_v$ except for one vertex $c_u$, which it violates. We call $u$ the current Achilles heel of $\alpha$.

Under these invariants, it is clear that Prover wins exactly when all edges incident to $u$ are in $\rho$ because then $\rho$ violates $c_u$ and thus violates exactly one of the $2^{\deg(u)-1}$ clauses of $F_u$.

For a partial assignment $\rho$, i.e., a state of the Atserias-Dalmau game, let $E_\rho$ denote the set of edges $e$ for which $x_e$ is defined by $\rho$, and let $G_{-\rho} := (V, E \setminus E_{\rho})$. That is, $G_{-\rho}$ is the graph obtained by removing edges defined under $\rho$.

Observation 1.4.2 When we remove up to $w+1$ edges from $G$, no connected component will have at size in $[t/2,t]$, i.e., each will have either fewer than $t/2$ vertices or more than $t$.

Proof Let $S \subseteq V$ be a connected component of $G_{-\rho}$. None of the edges in $\delta_G(S)$ is in $G_{-\rho}$ because $S$ is a connected component thereof; thus they are all defined under $\rho$, and $|\delta_G(S)| \leq w$. If $t/2 \leq |S| \leq t$ then by expansion we have $|\delta_G(S)| \geq h |S| \geq ht/2$. Thus $w \geq ht/2$, a contradiction to our assumption.A\(\square\)

Delayer now maintains a third invariant:

  1. The connected component of $G_{-\rho}$ containing the Achilles heel of $\alpha$ has more than $t$ vertices.

How can Delayer maintain the invariant? Let $S$ be the connected component of $G_{-\rho}$ containing the Achilles heel $u$ of $\alpha$. Suppose Prover queries $x_e$. There are two cases:

Case 1: The set $S$ is connected in $(V, E \setminus E_\rho \setminus \{e\})$, too. Then there is nothing to do. Delayer answers the query with $\alpha(x_e)$ and does not change $\alpha$. The size of the connected component containing $u$ does not shrink.

Case 2: Removing $e$ makes $S$ break into two parts $S_1$ and $S_2$. Since $t \lt |S| = |S|_1 + |S|_2$, at least one of the parts is greater than $t/2$, say $S_1$. Since $S_1$ is a component of the graph obtained from $G$ by removing $|\rho|+1 \leq w+1$ edges, we know from the claim that $|S_1| \gt t$. If the Achilles heel $u$ is in $S_1$ we simply answer $\rho(x_e) = \alpha(x_e$); the connected component containing $u$ shrinks, but is still greater than $t$. If $u \in S_2$ we have a problem since $S_2$ might become very small and eventually contain all edges around $x_e$. Delayer has to "escape": the chooses an arbitrary $v \in S_1$ and a path $p$ from $u$ to $v$ in $G_{-\rho}$. She constructs $\alpha' := \alpha \oplus p$, meaning taking $\alpha$ but flipping all $x_f$ for edges $f \in p$. She only changes variables undefined by $\rho$, so $\alpha'$ is still a superassignment of $\rho$. It satisfies all constraints except $v$. So Delayer changed $\alpha$ to $\alpha'$, which moved the Achilles heel into the large enough connected component $S_1$. Thus she maintains all invariants and can play indefinitely.A\(\square\)
