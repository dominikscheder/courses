1.3 Atserias-Dalmau Games

There is a different way of proving resolution width lower bounds, not reasoning about implication and non-implication (as we did in the previous chapter) but by modeling resolution as a game between a prover and a delayer. Loosely speaking, the pover wants to demonstrate that $F$ is unsatisfiable; the delayer wants to delay or completely avoid this. The prover proceeds by building up a partial assignment $\rho$ on the variables: in each round she picks a variable $x$ and asks the delayer What is the value of $x$? to which the delayer answers with a Boolean value $b \in \{0,1\}$, and the prover adds $[x \mapsto b]$ to $\rho$. The prover wins once $\rho$ violates a clause $C \in F$. If $F$ is unsatisfiable, she can surely win the game because once $\rho$ becomes a total assignment it must violate some clause. The point is that she has limited memory: she must make sure that $\rho$ is defined on at most $w$ variables at any point. More formally:

Algorithm 1.3.1 Atserias-Dalmau game $\adg(F,w)$:

  1. $\rho := [\ ]$, the empty assignment

  2. while no clause of $F$ is violated by $\rho$:

    1. Prover picks a variable $x$ of $V$.

    2. Delayer responds with a truth value $b \in \{0,1\}$.

    3. Prover sets $\rho := [x \mapsto b]$

    4. If $\rho$ contains more than $w+1$ variabels, prover must "forget" an assignment in $\rho$.

  3. Prover can now exhibit a clause $C \in F$ with $C(\rho) = 0$, winning the game.

Delayer wins if he can make sure that the while-loop never terminates. If every clause of $F$ has size exactly $k$ and $w \lt k$ then of course Delayer wins the game. His challenge is to win it even for "large" $w$, e.g. for $w = n/3$.

Theorem 1.3.2 If $F$ has a resolution refutation of width at most $w$ then prover can win $\adg(F,w)$.

Proof Let $C_1, C_2, \dots, C_l = \Box$ be a resolution refutation of $F$. The prover makes her way from $C_l$ at the end of the proof backwards to a clause of $F$, maintaining the invariant that her $\rho$ violates her current clause. Here is her strategy:

Algorithm 1.3.3 Strategy for Prover:

  1. $\rho = [\ ]$ in the beginning.

  2. Set $C = C_l = \Box$.

  3. while $C \not \in F$: # $\rho$ violates $C$ by invariant

    1. $C$ is a resolvent of two earlier clauses $D_1, D_2$ in the resolution refutation.

    2. Let $x$ be the resolved variable.

    3. Prover asks about $x$

    4. Delayer answers with $b \in \{0,1\}$.

    5. The new assignment $\rho \cup [x \mapsto b]$ violates exactly one clause of $D_1, D_2$, say without loss of generality $D_1$.

    6. Prover forgets all variables not in $D_1$, so $|\rho| = |D_1| \leq w$.

    7. Prover sets $C := D_1$.

  4. The while-loop ends because the position of clause $C$ in the resolution refutation $C_1, \dots, C_l$ moves to the left in each iteration.

Once $C \in F$, the invariant tells us that $C(\rho) = 0$, and thus Prover wins the game.A\(\square\)

This theorem is hugely useful: if we can define a strategy for Delayer to win the game on $(F,w)$, then every resolution refutation needs width more than $w$! The theorem has a converse, which is not only less useful but also slightly more difficult to prove:

Theorem 1.3.4 Suppose Prover can win the game $\adg(F,w)$, then there is a resolution refutation of $F$ of width at most $w$.

Proof First of all we add another derivation rule to our resolution calculus: if we have derived a clause $D$, we can derive a superclause $C \supseteq D$; this is called the weakening rule; it is clearly correct, and we can always get rid of weakening steps without increasing length of width of the proof. We will need a slightly more general statement. First we allow $\adg(F,w)$ to start with a partial assignment $\rho$ that is not empty (but surely $|\rho| \leq w$). We call this $\adg(F,w,\rho)$. The stronger statement now is:

Claim 1.3.5 If Prover can win $\adg(F,w,\rho)$ then there is a resolution proof $C_1, C_2, \dots, C_l$ from $F$ of width at most $w$ such that $\rho$ violates the final clause $C_l$.

If we can prove the claim, then the theorem follows by playing $\adg(F,w,[\ ])$: the assignment $[\ ]$ will violate the final clause $C_l$, which can only mean $C_l = \Box$. So let's prove the claim.

We prove the claim by induction on the length of the game, i.e., the number of times the players iterate through the while-loop. If Prover wins with 0 iterations of the loop, then the original $\rho$ violates some clause $C$ of $F$. So $C$ is by itself a resolution proof from $F$, of width $|C| \leq |\rho| \leq w$, ending in $C$, and $\rho$ violating its final clause.

So suppose Prover can win within $t$ steps, no matter the moves Delayer makes. Prover queries a variable $x$. Let $b \in \{0,1\}$ be the value Delayer answers. Prover builds $\rho \cup [x \mapsto b]$ and possibly forgets some other variable; call the resulting partial assignment $\rho_b$. Since Prover can win within $t$ steps no matter what Delayer answers, he can win within $t-1$ steps when starting with $\rho_0$ and when starting with $\rho_1$. In other words: she can win $\adg(F,w,\rho_0)$ within $t-1$ steps and $\adg(F,w,\rho_1)$ within $t-1$ steps.

By the induction hypothesis, there is a resultion proof $\pi_0$ from $F$ of width $w$ ending in $C_0$ such that $\rho_0$ violates $C_0$; similarly a proof $\pi_1$ ending in $C_1$ such that $C_1(\rho_1) = 0$. Suppose $x \not \in \var(C_1) \cap \var(C_0)$, say without loss of generality $x \not \in \var(C_0)$. Then already $\rho$ violates $C_0$ and the claim is proved, just taking proof $\pi_0$. Otherwise, $x$ appears in both clauses. Since $C_0(\rho_0)=0$ we conclude that $\rho_0(x)=0$ (it must be defined and surely it isn't 1), so $x \in C_0$. Similarly $\bar{x} \in C_1$. They have a resolvent which we call $C$. Every variable of $C$ is defined by $\rho$ and indeed $\rho(C) = 0$. Since $|\rho| \leq w$ this also implies $|C| \leq w$, finishing the proof.A\(\square\)
